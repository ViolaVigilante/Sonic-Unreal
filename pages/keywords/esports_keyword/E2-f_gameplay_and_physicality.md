    <p>One of the most common objections to esports as sports centers the physicality (or perceived lack thereof) in both categories. Esports cannot be real sports, this argument goes, because <em>real</em> sports involve significant physical exertion; esports, on the other hand, are just people sitting at computers.</p>
    <p>The manipulation of physical interfaces by bodily movements is, however, central to even the most “virtual” of digital media; even in technofuturist media like <em>The Matrix</em> and the <a class="popup-hyperlink" data-bs-toggle="popover" data-bs-html="true" data-bs-title="Explore Further" data-bs-content="<a href='/Sonic-Unreal/transhumanism'>Transhumanism</a>">transhumanist</a> aspirations of technologies like the neuralink, the body is still a necessary - if sublimated - interface itself. In other words: <a class="popup-comment" data-bs-toggle="popover" data-bs-html="true" data-bs-title="Embodiment & Digital Media" data-bs-content="Ladewig, Rebekka, and Henning Schmidgen. 2022. 
    <a target='_blank' href='https://doi.org/10.1177/1357034X221097068'>“Symmetries of Touch: Reconsidering Tactility in the Age of Ubiquitous Computing.”</a> <em>Body & Society</em> 28(1–2):3–23.<br><br>Parisi, David. 2018. <a target='_blank' href='https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517900595/archaeologies-of-touch/'><em>Archaeologies of Touch: Interfacing with Haptics from Electricity to Computing</em></a>. Minneapolis: University of Minnesota Press.">digital media, rather than <em>bypassing</em> the body, are accessed <em>through</em> the body</a>. Indeed, a growing body of literature in sports studies and human-computer interaction (HCI) attends to the physicality of esports play, <a class="popup-comment" data-bs-toggle="popover" data-bs-html="true" data-bs-title="Embodiment & Video Game Interfaces" data-bs-content="Biocca, Frank. 1997. <a target='_blank' href='https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1083-6101.1997.tb00070.x'>“The Cyborg’s Dilemma: Progressive Embodiment in Virtual Environments.”</a> <em>Journal of Computer-Mediated Communication</em> 3(2).<br><br>Blomberg, Johan. 2018. <a target='_blank' href='https://gamestudies.org/1802/articles/blomberg'>“The Semiotics of the Game Controller.”</a> <em>Game Studies</em> 18(2).<br><br>O’Grady, David. 2019. 
    <a target='_blank' href='https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.18574/nyu/9781479830404.003.0036/html'>“NES D-Pad: Interface.”</a> In <em>How to Play Video Games</em>, edited by Matthew Thomas Payne and Nina B. Huntemann, 269–76. New York: New York University Press.<br><br>Skalski, Paul, Ron Tamborini, Ashleigh Shelton, Michael Buncher, and Pete Lindmark. 2010. 
    <a target='_blank' href='https://doi.org/10.1177/1461444810370949'>“Mapping the Road to Fun: Natural Video Game Controllers, Presence, and Game Enjoyment.”</a> <em>New Media & Society</em> 13(2):224–42.<br><br>Summerley, Rory. 2025. <a target='_blank' href='https://doi.org/10.1123/jege.2024-0056'>“‘Modern Problems Require Modern Solutions’: An Autoethnography of Motor Impairment, Modern Controls in Street Fighter 6, and [Fighting] Game Accessibility.”</a> <em>Journal of Electronic Gaming and Esports</em> 3(1).">drawing on a preexisting body of work in HCI, game studies, and platform studies on bodily interfaces and video games more generally</a>.</p>

    <p>Top-level esports players train their bodies in a fashion similar to professional athletes  - if with a focus on different sets of strengths, endurances, and reflexes than, say, a swimmer or a football player. Esports organizations often provide similar support structures for their players as traditional sports teams, from <a class="popup-citation" onmouseover="myFunction(event)" onmouseout="myFunction(event)" href="https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2021.0109" target="_blank">physical therapists<span class="popuptext">McGee, Caitlin, Matthew Hwu, Leslie L. Nicholson, and Kevin K.N. Ho. 2021. “More Than a Game: Musculoskeletal Injuries and a Key Role for the Physical Therapist in Esports.” <em>Journal of Orthopaedic & Sports Physical Therapy</em> 51(9):415–17.</span></a> to <a class="popup-citation" onmouseover="myFunction(event)" onmouseout="myFunction(event)" href="https://www.ijesports.org/article/145/html" target="_blank">dietitians<span class="popuptext">Lawson, Kate, and James Fleming. 2025. “Time to ‘Power-Up’ Esports Performance Nutrition: A Systematic Scoping Review.” <em>International Journal of Esports</em> 1(1).</span></a> to <a class="popup-citation" onmouseover="myFunction(event)" onmouseout="myFunction(event)" href="https://doi.org/10.1080/21520704.2018.1518280" target="_blank">sports psychologists<span class="popuptext">Cottrell, Cory, Nicholas McMillen, and Brandonn S. Harris. 2019. “Sport Psychology in a Virtual World: Considerations for Practitioners Working in eSports.” <em>Journal of Sport Psychology in Action</em> 10(2):73–81.</span></a>. Success in professional esports is dependent on specific bodily capacities for which specific training regimens exist, and bodily changes from injuries and aging are a common reason for players’ exists from the profession. There are even similar concerns in both esports and traditional sports around performance enhancing drugs - though <a class="popup-citation" onmouseover="myFunction(event)" onmouseout="myFunction(event)" href="https://www.taylorfrancis.com/chapters/edit/10.4324/9781003258650-10/doping-esports-joanna-wall-tweedie-rebecca-rosenthal-john-holden" target="_blank">there tends to be significantly less regulation<span class="popuptext">Tweedie, Joanna Wall, Rebecca R. Rosenthal, and John T. Holden. 2022. “Doping in Esports.” In <em>Social Issues in Esports</em>, edited by Anne Tjønndal. New York: Routledge.</span></a> of their use in esports.</p>
    <p>Now, all of this is not to argue for an equivalency between the physicality involved in playing a game of basketball and the physicality involved when playing a game of <a class="popup-hyperlink" data-bs-toggle="popover" data-bs-html="true" data-bs-title="Explore Further" data-bs-content="<a href='/Sonic-Unreal/league-of-legends'><em>League of Legends</em></a>"><em>League of Legends</em></a>; it is, however, to argue that esports play is physical in nature, if differently physical from many traditional sports. Indeed, the embodied skills required to successfully compete in some traditional sports might have more in common with those required for many esports than with other traditional sports. The hand-eye coordination required of top-tier air rifle shooters, for example, may be closer to the skills required to play <em>League of Legends</em> than those required for, say, American football (and not to mention what are often called <a class="popup-hyperlink" data-bs-toggle="popover" data-bs-html="true" data-bs-title="Explore Further" data-bs-content='<a href="/Sonic-Unreal/what-are-sports-anyway">What are "Sports," Anyway?</a>'>“lifestyle sports”</a> such as darts and bowling).</p>
